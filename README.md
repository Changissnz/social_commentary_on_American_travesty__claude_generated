## All Madness Seems To Point to America

*A Social Critique*

### I. The New Rome

There is a peculiar vanity that afflicts great powers at the height of their confidence: they begin to see themselves reflected in antiquity. America is no exception. In the corridors of its think tanks, in the speeches of its senators, and in the strategic ambitions of its foreign policy establishment, the ghost of the Roman Empire has become a recurring specter. Comparisons between American political figures and Roman leaders --- Caesar, Augustus, Caligula --- have moved from academic metaphor to mainstream political vocabulary.

As historians and commentators noted with growing urgency through 2024, the parallels being drawn were not merely literary flourishes. The rhetoric of \"crossing the Rubicon,\" of a Republic tipping into Empire, of a Senate reduced to theater --- these were offered not as warnings but, in some quarters, as aspirations. One historian, writing in TIME Magazine, observed that conflicts between populist politicians and the conservative establishment had \"spiraled from legislative gridlock to the politicization of the criminal justice system,\" and identified this as a pattern Rome had walked before its transformation into autocracy.\[1\] The Roman Senate, as scholars remind us, did not vanish overnight; it was preserved as the legitimating institution of an order it no longer controlled.

What is striking is not merely that outside critics apply the Roman lens to America --- it is that many within the American political class have internalized it. To position oneself as Caesar is to understand that the Republic is already past saving. It is to frame raw power as historical destiny. The language of empire, when adopted by the powerful, is never simply description. It is permission.

*\[1\] Jeffrey E. Schulman, \"The U.S. Faces Risks Ancient Rome Faced in Caesar\'s Day,\" TIME, March 2024.*

### II. The Equality Racket

The founding doctrine of the American experiment is equality --- a proposition radical enough, in the eighteenth century, to justify revolution. But a doctrine is not a practice, and between the declaration that all men are created equal and the ordering of actual society, there has always existed a profitable gap. In the American system, equality was proclaimed universally and administered selectively, and the distance between those two facts has generated enormous wealth for those skilled at managing it.

The machinery is elegant in its brutality: assert equality as an absolute; then find the differences --- racial, geographic, ideological, national --- that can be weaponized against one another. War is the most reliable instrument. As former President Dwight D. Eisenhower warned in his 1961 farewell address, the emergence of a permanent armaments industry created systemic pressure toward perpetual conflict. He called it \"the military-industrial complex\" --- an alliance of defense contractors, the armed forces, and the congressional interests that fed both --- and cautioned that its potential for \"disastrous misplaced power\" would \"exist and persist.\"\[2\] He was correct. More than sixty years on, America finds itself in what critics have described as a state of perpetual war, and perpetual war generates perpetual profits.

The deep logic is this: equality is most profitable when it is contested. Equal nations may trade; unequal ones can be made to fight. The universalism of American values --- freedom, democracy, the rights of man --- has functioned not only as a moral compass but as an export product, and like all export products, it is most lucrative when demand can be manufactured.

*\[2\] President Dwight D. Eisenhower, Farewell Address to the Nation, January 17, 1961. National Archives.*

### III. The Public Sector and the Rot Within

The vision of the state as guarantor of social welfare --- the Bismarckian inheritance filtered through progressive American politics --- has always collided, in America, with a deep cultural distrust of government competence. The result is an institution designed to be mediocre: underfunded, understaffed, structurally subordinate to private capital, and periodically raided by the political class for patronage.

What follows from this arrangement is predictable. Where public services atrophy, private capital rushes to fill the vacuum --- but not uniformly. It clusters. It concentrates in markets where return is highest and risk is lowest, abandoning the peripheries to whatever scraps the state still provides. The geography of American prosperity is, in this sense, a map of where private capital has decided it is comfortable. Silicon Valley, the financial corridors of Manhattan, the defense hubs of Northern Virginia --- these are not accidents. They are the sediment of capital choosing its habitats.

The public sector, in this configuration, functions less as a system of social insurance than as a subsidy machine: its failures justify privatization; its contracts enrich the private firms that replace it; its regulatory capture ensures that the industries it nominally oversees write their own rules. The corruption this breeds is not the operatic kind, of briefcases and backrooms, but the structural kind --- the slow institutional degradation that comes from designing a public administration to be permanently second-rate.

### IV. The Leaders and Their Fictions

A ruling class that governs by disinformation does not necessarily begin in cynicism. It often begins in convenience --- in the decision, made at a critical juncture, that the truth is inconvenient. The Iraq War provides the paradigm case. The decision to invade was not driven by a surplus of intelligence; it was driven by a deficit of honesty about the intelligence available. Senior officials promoted \"unfounded intelligence about Iraqi WMD\" and pressured analytical agencies to tailor their findings to fit a predetermined policy.\[3\]

What makes this not merely a scandal but a civilizational symptom is the mechanism of transmission. Disinformation succeeds because it finds willing carriers in trusted institutions --- media organizations, intelligence assessments, the testimony of credentialed officials. The American public was not simply lied to; it was systematically misinformed by a cascade of actors, some deceived themselves, others knowingly complicit. By 2002, polling showed that 77 percent of Americans believed Iraq was developing weapons of mass destruction.\[4\] The infrastructure of public trust had been turned against the public.

This is the pattern that repeats: not a single villain fabricating lies from nothing, but a system that processes convenient falsehoods upward through institutional channels until they acquire the patina of established fact. The middle managers of power --- the briefers, the spokespersons, the think tank fellows --- are not always liars. They are, more often, readers of incentives. In an environment that rewards consensus and punishes dissent, the truth requires extraordinary courage to speak. Most choose otherwise.

*\[4\] Nuclear Network / CSIS, \"Nuclear Mis- and Disinformation and Impacts to the American Public,\" April 2024.*

### V. Torture, Robbery, and the Civilizational Substrate

Beneath every great civilization lies a stratum of violence. America is not exceptional in this respect, but it is exceptional in its insistence on its own exceptionalism --- in the peculiar energy it devotes to not looking at what lies beneath. The history of American civilization is also a history of land seizure, of the forced labor of enslaved people, of the dispossession of indigenous nations, of the wars waged against the poor in the name of order.

These are not ancient grievances, sealed by time. Their consequences persist in the present distribution of wealth, in the architecture of cities, in the composition of who serves in the military and who does not, in who owns property and who rents. A nation that refuses to examine its foundational violences does not thereby escape them. It simply reencounters them in new forms --- as mass incarceration, as surveillance, as the permanent emergency that justifies extraordinary measures.

The United States holds more of its own citizens in prison than any other nation on earth. This is not incidental to American civilization; it is constitutive of a particular model of social control. Imprisonment functions, among other things, as a mechanism for removing from circulation those who cannot be profitably incorporated into the economy. The carceral state is the inheritor, in attenuated form, of older instruments of coercive labor. To call this robbery is not hyperbole; it is description.

### VI. Scientists and the Tyranny of Capital

America\'s relationship with its scientists has always been one of conditional toleration. The scientist is useful when producing the technologies of power --- the bomb, the satellite, the pharmaceutical --- and marginal when her conclusions inconvenience the industries that fund her. The history of American science is, in part, a history of this tension: between the imperatives of inquiry and the imperatives of investment.

Eisenhower himself, in that same prophetic farewell address, warned of a second danger alongside the military-industrial complex: the \"domination of the nation\'s scholars by Federal project allocations,\" and the risk that \"public policy could itself become the captive of a scientific-technological elite.\" What he perhaps did not fully anticipate was the inverse problem --- not science dominating policy, but capital dominating science. The research university, the national laboratory, the independent scientist: all are dependent on funding that comes with conditions, explicit or implicit.

The result is a science that is technically brilliant and institutionally compromised --- capable of remarkable discoveries, systematically constrained in what discoveries it is permitted to make consequential. Climate science provides the most glaring example: the evidence has been overwhelming for decades; the policy response has been calibrated to what the fossil fuel industry will permit. The scientists were not wrong. They were simply outgunned.

### VII. The Art of the Deal and the Poverty of Business Thought

Donald Trump\'s 1987 book The Art of the Deal is, in one sense, a minor document of the Reagan era --- a celebrity businessman\'s account of his own genius. In another sense, it is an unusually candid manual for a certain kind of operator: buy when you have the money, extract maximum return from market speculation, project confidence regardless of the underlying reality. Its recipe for success is essentially a recipe for socially legitimized aggression, dressed in the language of positive thinking.

The book\'s lasting significance is not biographical but diagnostic. Its principles --- leveraged speculation, the primacy of the deal over the relationship, the weaponization of optimism as a substitute for truth --- are not peculiar to Trump. They describe the operating logic of a significant portion of American business culture across the political spectrum. The venture capitalist who funds vaporware startups, the private equity firm that extracts value from failing companies, the pharmaceutical executive who reprices essential medications: all are practicing variants of the same epistemology. Acquire. Extract. Declare victory.

What this mode of thought systematically undervalues is the long-term, the collective, and the true. It is constitutionally incapable of addressing problems that require sustained investment, honest accounting, and the willingness to deliver on commitments that may cost more than they return. Infrastructure. Public health. Education. Climate. The Art of the Deal is useless in the face of all of them --- and yet its logic governs the institutions that must address them.

### VIII. The Information Age and Its Discontents

The utopian promise of the Information Age was democratic: the free flow of knowledge would empower citizens, hold power accountable, and dissolve the barriers that concentrated authority. The reality has been more complex. The same architectures that distribute information also distribute disinformation. The same platforms that amplify dissent also amplify derangement. And the data that flows through these systems is not neutral; it is owned, monetized, and leveraged by actors whose interests diverge sharply from those of the users who generate it.

Privacy, in this context, has ceased to function as a right and has become a commodity --- something surrendered in exchange for services, or extracted without consent by institutions public and private. The surveillance capitalism described by theorists has matured into a permanent condition of modern life: behavioral prediction, algorithmic management, the continuous sifting of human activity for signals of commercial and political utility. The \"extrajudicial\" dimension --- the ways in which platforms adjudicate speech, identity, and access outside any legal framework --- adds a further layer of unaccountable power.

The fear that pervades the Information Age is not the fear of ignorance but of visibility: of being seen, categorized, and acted upon by systems whose criteria are invisible and whose appeals processes do not exist. In the United States, where the regulatory framework for digital platforms remains rudimentary, this fear is not irrational. The Information Age did not abolish power. It gave power new instruments.

### IX. The AI Bull Market and the Decline of Thought

The artificial intelligence gold rush of the 2020s has revealed something important about how America processes technological transformation: it speculates first and thinks later. Investment in AI data center infrastructure reached half a trillion dollars globally in 2024, with the United States accounting for the largest share of electricity consumption in the sector --- approximately 45 percent of the world total.\[5\] The scale of commitment precedes, by some distance, any serious public reckoning with what is being built, for whom, and at what cost.

The environmental costs alone are staggering in their implications. U.S. data centers consumed 183 terawatt-hours of electricity in 2024 --- more than 4 percent of the country\'s total electricity consumption, roughly equivalent to the entire annual demand of Pakistan.\[6\] By 2030, this figure is projected to more than double. The electricity driving AI inference and training is generated, in significant part, by fossil fuels; the carbon footprint of AI systems could reach between 32.6 and 79.7 million tons of CO2 in 2025 alone.\[7\] In some regions, ordinary consumers are already paying higher electricity bills to subsidize the expansion of data centers that serve hyperscale tech companies.

But the more insidious cost is intellectual. The AI bull market has produced a culture in which engineers become evangelists, investors become philosophers, and the serious work of understanding what these systems actually do --- their biases, their failure modes, their political economy --- is crowded out by promotional noise. Scholarship is devalued; idolatry toward whoever controls the most compute is pronounced. STEM, once a domain of disciplined inquiry, now presents its public face through the figures of the speculator and the marketer. The scientists remain, somewhere in the back rooms, still doing the actual work. They are simply not the ones who get to speak.

*\[6\] Pew Research Center, \"US data centers\' energy use amid the artificial intelligence boom,\" October 2025.*

*\[7\] Masanet et al., \"The carbon and water footprints of data centers,\" ScienceDirect, 2025.*

### Coda: The Madness and Its Logic

To call all this madness is not to say it is irrational. Every pathology described here has its logic, its beneficiaries, its internal consistency. The military-industrial complex is profitable. The disinformation ecosystem is functional. The AI bubble enriches its early participants. The carceral state keeps certain populations controlled. The empire delusion provides psychological compensation for a political class that senses its legitimacy slipping.

What makes it madness is the aggregate --- the convergence of these pathologies into a system that has become, measurably, incapable of addressing its own most urgent problems. A civilization that governs by fable, profits from manufactured conflict, subordinates science to investment, and confuses speculation with thought is not prepared for the challenges it faces. The Roman comparison, for all its overuse, captures something real: not that America is Rome, but that the pattern of imperial self-deception, of bread and circus, of legal formalism masking authoritarian drift, does not care about the nationality of those who enact it.

The madness, in other words, is not specifically American. It is the madness of power that has decided it no longer needs to tell the truth. America is simply its most consequential current address.

## The Caucasian Bastards Thought

*Social Commentary on the Age of Protectionism*

March 2026

**I. The Weight of Accumulated Crises**

In the quarter-century since September 11, 2001, the United States has
lurched from one civilizational stress test to the next --- wars of
dubious mandate, a catastrophic financial collapse, a pandemic of
historic proportion, and now a protectionist retrenchment that promises
to reshape the global order. These cascading crises have not affected
Americans equally. They have fallen, as crises always do, most heavily
on those at the margins.

Among those margins, one finds the so-called \"model minorities\" --- a
designation the American media applies with curious enthusiasm to
certain immigrant and ethnic communities, particularly East and South
Asians. The term, first deployed in the 1960s as a political
counterweight against Black civil rights activism, has endured as a
rhetorical convenience. It papers over the full spectrum of human
behavior within those communities, flattening millions of individuals
into a single admirable archetype. What it obscures is more revealing
than what it acknowledges: the corruption, the moral compromise, the
petty tribalism and the grave transgressions that exist in every
community of sufficient size and economic pressure.

The standards applied to \"model\" communities are, of course,
calibrated to be achievable. Selective statistical narratives --- median
household income, educational attainment --- are deployed to construct
an image of exemplary assimilation, while structural disadvantages,
intra-community poverty, and social dysfunction are rendered invisible.
The model minority myth does not celebrate; it instrumentalizes.

> of the Model Minority (Princeton University Press, 2014); also Robert
> S. Chang, \"Toward an Asian American Legal Scholarship,\" 81
> California Law Review 1241 (1993).*

**II. The Normalization of Manufactured Untruth**

The epistemological degradation of American public life did not begin
with COVID-19, but the pandemic accelerated it with terrifying
efficiency. What had been, in the years of Donald Trump\'s first term, a
crisis substantially attributed to foreign interference --- Russian
information operations, the Internet Research Agency\'s systematic
seeding of social division --- became, after 2020, a domestic condition.
Misinformation and disinformation are no longer aberrations. They are
the ambient atmosphere of American political discourse.

The mechanism is not difficult to understand. The democratization of
information technology --- social media platforms, algorithmic content
distribution, the collapse of local journalism --- has dramatically
lowered the cost of publishing falsehood while simultaneously destroying
the gatekeeping infrastructure that once, imperfectly, checked it. A
claim that would have required a broadcast license or a printing press
to disseminate in 1985 now requires only a smartphone and a grievance.

Into this environment, Western governments have introduced incoherence
at scale: contradictory immigration postures that alienate both nativist
and cosmopolitan constituencies; penal philosophies that oscillate
between mass incarceration and sudden decarceration without addressing
the underlying conditions that generate crime; banking regulations that,
since the 2008 crisis, have been revised, relaxed, and weaponized in
service of successive administrations\' political priorities. The
incoherence is not accidental. It reflects the genuine difficulty of
governing pluralist societies under conditions of accelerating
technological change --- but it reads to frustrated citizens as
betrayal, and betrayal breeds the appetite for simple explanations.

**III. The Plebian Turn: Moralism, Witch Hunts, and the Architecture of
Cover-Up**

A society that has lost confidence in its elite institutions does not,
as a rule, replace them with superior ones. It replaces them with cruder
ones. What the present moment has produced is a surge of plebian
moralism --- a politics of grievance and denunciation that is
emotionally powerful and analytically shallow, that identifies villains
with speed and precision while remaining studiously incurious about
structural causes.

The witch hunt --- a term not deployed here as metaphor but as
descriptive category --- has become a primary instrument of political
competition. It operates largely outside formal legal channels: through
social media pile-ons, coordinated harassment campaigns, selective leaks
to sympathetic journalists, and the extrajudicial application of
reputational destruction. These operations carry the hallmarks of
intelligence tradecraft --- surveillance, sexual leverage, the
cultivation of informants --- deployed not by state actors against
foreign adversaries but by domestic political factions against domestic
targets. The law has not kept pace with these practices. Their victims,
whether guilty of something or nothing, bear costs that the perpetrators
do not.

The cover-up is the witch hunt\'s logical companion. Where institutions
have reason to conceal --- whether to protect powerful individuals,
preserve organizational reputation, or avoid the legal consequences of
past decisions --- transparency becomes the enemy. Significant portions
of public administrative capacity in the contemporary United States are
devoted not to service delivery but to the management of embarrassing
information. This is not a partisan observation; it describes the
behavior of institutions across the ideological spectrum.

**IV. The Automation Paradox and the Mythology of Meritocracy**

The United States has long organized its civic mythology around work:
the Protestant ethic, the self-made man, the immigrant who arrives with
nothing and builds everything. This mythology performs important
ideological work. It naturalizes inequality by attributing economic
outcomes to individual virtue, and it sustains political consent by
implying that the system is, at bottom, fair.

Automation is doing to this mythology what the Industrial Revolution did
to the craftsman\'s guild: rendering it structurally obsolete while its
emotional power endures. The tertiary sector --- services, broadly
construed --- now accounts for the overwhelming majority of American
employment. But the service economy is not uniformly accessible. Its
upper registers, the credentialed professions that confer social status
and economic security, are protected by elaborate systems of credential
gatekeeping, professional licensing, and what economists euphemistically
term \"network effects\" --- which is to say, who you know, where you
went to school, what your parents did, and what you look like.

A nation that imports semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, consumer
electronics, and increasingly food cannot credibly claim that its
residents\' failure to achieve professional status reflects individual
inadequacy rather than structural constraint. The pipeline from
childhood to credentialed adulthood is long, expensive, and riddled with
points of failure that are not randomly distributed. They cluster along
lines of race, class, geography, and parental wealth. Racial favoritism
and aristocratic nepotism --- terms that sound impolite in polite
company but describe observable phenomena in the hiring and admissions
data --- mean that intelligence and diligence are necessary but not
sufficient conditions for professional success.

**V. Trade Policy, Technological Fragmentation, and the North-South
Fault Line**

American trade policy in the high-technology sector has arrived, after
decades of export-led globalization, at a moment of defensive
nationalism that has introduced new incoherencies even as it attempts to
resolve old ones. The effort to curtail the market dominance of Big Tech
--- pursued through antitrust action, regulatory pressure, and
legislative threat --- has coincided with, and arguably contributed to,
a contraction of domestic production capacity in precisely the sectors
that a technology-intensive economy requires.

The tariff regime that has accumulated across successive administrations
has fractured supply chains into an awkward plurality: local chains that
lack the scale and specialized labor to compete with established
offshore producers; national chains that depend on federal subsidy and
are therefore subject to political discontinuity; and foreign chains
that, despite official discouragement, persist because they remain
cheaper. The CHIPS and Science Act of 2022, whatever its merits as
industrial policy, illustrates the difficulty: billions allocated to
semiconductor fabrication capacity, with implementation timelines
measured in years, against a global competitive environment that moves
in months.

Underlying all of this is a geography that the language of national
unity tends to suppress: the American north-south divide, which is not
merely historical residue but a living structural condition. The
stereotypes have empirical correlates. Northern metropolitan economies
--- concentrated in finance, technology, higher education, and media ---
operate on fundamentally different premises than the extractive,
agricultural, and manufacturing economies of the interior South. The
latter\'s aristocratic traditionalism is not merely cultural; it is
embedded in land tenure patterns, labor market structures, and political
economy arrangements that have resisted reform for generations. When
national trade policy is formulated in Washington, it is formulated
primarily by and for the interests of the former.

**VI. Identity Politics, Classism, and the Harvest of Ugly Fruit**

Identity politics and its twin, classism, have by now demonstrated their
capacity to produce bad outcomes for everyone involved. The proponent of
identity-based political mobilization discovers, eventually, that
coalition politics fractures along precisely the lines of difference it
sought to transcend; that the symbolic victories achievable through this
mode of politics --- representation, recognition, the renaming of
institutions --- do not translate automatically into material
improvement for the communities they nominally serve; and that the
resentments generated by the politics of group hierarchy are durable and
politically exploitable by actors with no interest in justice of any
kind.

The opponent of identity politics, meanwhile, typically finds themselves
defending arrangements of privilege that they would prefer not to
examine too closely, deploying a universalism that is, on inspection,
highly selective --- applied with rigor to challenges from below and
with remarkable flexibility to the inherited advantages of the
already-established.

What both poles share is a fundamental incuriosity about class --- about
the material conditions of economic life, the distribution of productive
assets, the mechanisms by which advantage replicates itself across
generations. Class analysis is the missing term in American political
discourse, displaced alternately by race and by culture, leaving a
politics that generates enormous heat and very little light, and an
economics that proceeds largely on its own terms, indifferent to the
symbolic battles conducted in its shadow.

This is the age of protectionism --- not merely in trade, but in
ideology, in epistemology, in social organization. Walls are being built
everywhere: around information, around professions, around communities,
around nations. The walls keep some things out. They also keep the
builders in.
